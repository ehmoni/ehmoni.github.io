About Me

Bridging Industry
& Academic Research

"A Journey of a thousand miles begins with a single step." — Laozi

I am a passionate computer science researcher who started as a technology enthusiast undergraduate and went through a long journey with multiple track records in different fields — both in industry and academia — and still believe "I have miles to go, before I sleep."

Along this journey, I have acquired diverse experiences ranging from Computing & IT (Software Development, Networking) to Telecommunications (Network Operations, Quality, Automation) and then Business (Process, Six Sigma, Management), and finally Data Science (Databases, AI, and Machine Learning).

I like to take new challenges and embark on new ventures so I can acquire new knowledge and, by integrating them with previous insights, deploy them for better results. In the organizations I've worked with, I always encouraged innovative ideas and inspired problem-solving effectively — increasing my confidence to learn, teach, and build a stronger next generation.

At the University of Waterloo (Data Systems Group), my research falls broadly under AI — specifically at the cross-section of Knowledge Representation and Databases. I am supervised by Dr. Grant Weddell and Dr. David Toman.

Areas of Inquiry

My research sits at the intersection of AI and data management, exploring formal foundations with practical impact.

Poster · 01 · Data Integration
Structured Data Integration with GRM/SQLP

This project explores a new approach to structured data integration by extending traditional relational models into a graph-based framework. We introduce GRM/SQLP, a more expressive and flexible query language that separates schema design from reference resolution.


The work focuses on improving how heterogeneous data sources are combined, especially when dealing with identity and reference inconsistencies. By leveraging graph semantics, this approach can better represent complex relationships across datasets.


Overall, the project aims to make data integration more scalable, intuitive, and robust.

Poster · 02 · Knowledge Representation
Reliable Reference Mechanisms for Knowledge Bases

This research investigates how to maintain reliable references within description logic (DL) knowledge bases, especially under data updates. We develop algorithms and frameworks that address identity resolution challenges, ensuring consistency when data evolves over time.


The work combines theoretical foundations with practical mechanisms to manage references more effectively. A key contribution is improving how knowledge systems adapt to changes without losing correctness.


This helps support more dependable reasoning in dynamic data environments.

Poster · 03 · Parallel Computing
Parallel Graph Pattern Matching with Golang

In this project, I developed a graph pattern matching system using Golang to study the performance benefits of concurrency. The system, GraphMatch, implements subgraph matching using parallel goroutines and communication channels.


The research evaluates how factors like core count, goroutine scaling, and channel size impact performance in combinatorial problems. Results highlight both the strengths and limitations of parallelism, showing where concurrency improves efficiency and where overhead becomes significant.


This work contributes to understanding how concurrent programming can be applied to graph algorithms.

Poster · 04 · Data Quality
Data Cleaning in Ontological Multidimensional Models

This project focuses on improving data quality in multidimensional ontological systems by identifying and resolving inconsistencies. We propose a framework that uses logical constraints and weighted repairs to determine the most efficient fixes to problematic data.


By modeling data quality as a context-dependent problem, we leverage Answer Set Programming to compute optimal repairs. The approach considers both data and schema-level inconsistencies, offering a principled way to clean complex datasets.


Ultimately, this work aims to make analytical systems more reliable by ensuring their underlying data is consistent.
